SANS 10400-XA Simplifications

Explore how the Deemed-to-Satisfy (DTS) rules translate complex thermodynamic principles into rigid, prescriptive checklists.

Solar Orientation Categories
The Code's Assumption

Groups multiple compass directions into broad buckets for vertical fenestration (e.g. grouping West, North-West, North, North-East, and East orientations into one single category).

Thermodynamic Reality

Facades experience vastly different thermal loads degree-by-degree. The harsh, low-angle afternoon sun of a West-facing window is treated the same as the mild morning sun of an East-facing window.

The Silo Approach (No Trade-offs)
The Code's Assumption

Elements must satisfy requirements independently. If fenestration performance exceeds requirements on one storey, it cannot be traded off against a poorer performing second storey.

Thermodynamic Reality

Building physics relies on a holistic energy balance. Over-insulating a roof naturally requires less wall insulation to achieve the same overall thermal efficiency for the entire envelope.

Generic Occupancy Profiles
The Code's Assumption

Standardizes internal heat gains (people, lights, plug loads) into rigid weekday schedules. For example, assuming office lighting operates at exactly 80% from 08:00 to 11:00.

Thermodynamic Reality

Modern buildings have dynamic, varied uses. These tables cannot account for modern realities like hybrid work schedules, varied lunch hours, or specific energy-intensive tenant operations.

Ignoring Internal Blinds
The Code's Assumption

Mandates permanent external features (like eaves or fixed canopies) to block 80% of summer solar radiation. Internal curtains and blinds are given absolutely no insulation or shading value.

Thermodynamic Reality

While internal blinds reflect some light, once radiation passes through glass it becomes trapped inside as heat (the greenhouse effect). Furthermore, they rely entirely on unpredictable occupant behavior.

Window Condensation Bans
The Code's Assumption

Outright prohibits using single low-emissivity glass in the Southern Cape Condensation Problem Area to achieve required thermal U-values.

Thermodynamic Reality

Condensation is dynamic and depends heavily on indoor humidity, internal temperature, and ventilation rates. The model uses a blanket ban instead of requiring localized dew-point calculations.

Thermal Mass Thresholds
The Code's Assumption

Splits external wall requirements into two rigid categories based on a hard cutoff: surface density of ≥ 270 kg/m² versus < 270 kg/m².

Thermodynamic Reality

Ignores the nuanced thermal lag variations between materials. A heavy masonry wall behaves entirely differently than a lightweight timber-frame wall throughout a day-night temperature cycle.

Prescriptive Airtightness
The Code's Assumption

Assumes the building is adequately sealed by basic methods: caulking joints, adding skirting, installing tile underlays, and fitting compressible strips to external doors.

Thermodynamic Reality

Specifying weather stripping does not guarantee a truly airtight envelope. The model requires no physical validation (like a Blower Door test) to prove the building is actually sealed.

Hot Water (The 50% Volume Rule)
The Code's Assumption

Mandates that at least 50% (by volume fraction) of the annual average hot water requirement must be provided by alternative means like solar arrays or heat pumps.

Thermodynamic Reality

Assumes that replacing 50% of the volume equates to a 50% reduction in energy usage. It ignores that system efficiency fluctuates wildly; a solar system's EER can drop to 20% if not maintained.

Commercial Lighting Thresholds
The Code's Assumption

Restricts lighting energy by imposing a flat Watt per square meter (W/m²) limit averaged across the net floor area (e.g., 8 W/m² for Offices).

Thermodynamic Reality

Averages out the load across the entire floorplate. It fails to distinguish between areas requiring high-intensity task lighting and areas needing only low-level ambient lighting.

HVAC Efficiency Ratings
The Code's Assumption

Air conditioning compliance is achieved strictly by ensuring the unit meets a minimum Coefficient of Performance (COP) under specific, static laboratory test conditions.

Thermodynamic Reality

Evaluates the machine in isolation, not the system. It completely ignores real-world efficiency losses caused by leaky ductwork, part-load inefficiencies, or suboptimal thermostat placement.
